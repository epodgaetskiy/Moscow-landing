Welcome to Russia

Russia is at once breathtakingand baffling... where the ruling party downwards functions in its own, impenetrably Russian, way.

Russia is a country that was unreachable for many years behind a shroud of secrets and memory. Since the collapse of the Iron Curtain, Russia has opened its doors. It is now a welcoming place eager to connect with foreigners and share its unique heritage with those who visit.

One-seventh of the world’s landmass, Russia is the largest country on earth. In fact, by jet from Moscow, it takes about eight hours to reach Vladivostok on the Pacific Ocean coast. This expansive area allows it to hold a vast and complicated array of landscapes with a diverse blend of forty national parks and forty UNESCO Biosphere Reserves.

Russia is at once breathtaking and baffling. Monumental in every respect, it’s a land where burnished imperial splendor coexists with icy Siberian tundra, where timeworn Soviet-era monuments backdrop uber-hip urban cultures and where everything from the ruling party downwards functions in its own, impenetrably Russian, way.

Russia has made a priceless contribution to the world culture. It has given to the world not only great classics and fine arts masterpieces but entire schools. Russian drama school of Stanislavski is world-famous, and Russian ballet, its rich traditions and famous names of the ballet dancers are the most important cultural symbols of Russia.

The west of the country draws the most visitor attention, thanks to the presence of two extraordinary cities. St Petersburg and Moscow serve up sweeping postcard sights by the dozen. Moscow is the rapidly beating heart of the “New Russia,” where Asia and Europe combine to create a boisterous, enigmatic metropolis on a grand scale. St Petersburg, meanwhile, with its living film-set of palaces, cathedrals and waterways, is the grandest and most European of Russia’s cities, yet still retains a deeply complex character.

You’ve probably heard a lot of “facts”, myths and stereotypes about Russia. So: is it true that Russians drink vodka like it’s going out of fashion? Is it really cold here? And if you go to Russia, should you watch out for bears? The only way to find out which Russian “stereotypes” are actually true is to come to Russia! By all means, you will be doubly rewarded by the cultural riches of the country and the warmth of the Russian people.

High culture... jaw-dropping art exhibitions and an energetic live music scene continue to prove that Moscow is really on the cutting edge.

Moscow is the huge, historic, versatile, chaotic and beautiful capital of our country. A city of contrasts, Moscow’s busy streets all bear witness to Russia’s turbulent history and dynamic future.

From the medieval Kremlin, it is a fortified complex at the heart of Moscow, overlooking the Moskva River to the south, Saint Basil’s Cathedral and Red Square to the east, and the Alexander Garden to the west. The magical golden domes of Moscow’s beautiful churches, to its tranquil parks, monumental Soviet architecture and modern skyscrapers, the city’s skyline tells a thousand stories.

High culture courtesy of the Moscow Philharmonic and the Bolshoi Theatre, jaw-dropping art exhibitions and an energetic live music scene continue to prove that Moscow is really on the cutting edge. Whether you are looking for medieval history, communist nostalgia or high-rolling glamour, Moscow has something for everyone.

It’s said that if Moscow is Russia is head, St Petersburg is its heart. For 300 years this has been the country’s cultural capital, producing authors, poets, painters and playwrights.

It remains a bustling hub for the arts, showcasing everything from painting and literature to street theatre and rebel rock. The historic heart of this timeless, romantic city is focused on the crescent of land bound by the Neva and Fontanki Rivers. Here you will find the Admiralty, the Hermitage and Dvortsovaya Ploshchad (Palace Square), as well as Nevsky Prospekt, St Petersburg’s most elegant avenue. Focal points include the Mariinsky Theatre, west of the Admiralty, home to the famous Kirov ballet company.

North across the river are Vasilyevsky Island, with its universities and museums, and Petrogradskaya Storona, home to the Peter and Paul Fortress and even more famous museums. The Hermitage, the world’s largest art museum, founded by Catherine the Great in 1764. Today the collection of the State Hermitage includes more than three million works of art and artefacts of the world culture.

Fans are in for a memorable time, particularly as it will coincide with the city’s famed “White Nights” season, when the sun barely sets from mid-June to early July.

Peter and Paul Fortress

Lacking the fame and tourist awareness of the Hermitage or the Mariinsky Theatre, the Peter and Paul Fortress is certainly no less of a St. Petersburg landmark.

The first structure to be built in St. Petersburg, and thus the birthplace of the city, it never served its intended defensive function. Instead it has had a rich, hugely varied, and sometimes sinister history as a military base, as a home of government departments, the burial ground of the Russian Imperial family, the site of groundbreaking scientific experiments and a forbidding jail that held some of Russia’s most prominent political prisoners. Today, the Peter and Paul Fortress is for the most part under the auspices of the St. Petersburg Museum of History, with a number of permanent and temporary exhibitions charting the various aspects of the compound’s past. The Peter and Paul Fortress is also the centre of a number of St. Petersburg urban traditions, among them the daily firing of the cannon from the Naryshkin Bastion at noon and the “walruses” who use the beach in front of the fortress to sunbathe and swim in ice-holes in the winter.

The Kremlin

A potent symbol of two mighty imperial cultures — that of medieval Muscovy and that of the Soviet Union — the Kremlin is at once fascinating and foreboding, a mixture of lavish opulence and austere secrecy, and its eclectic mix of architecture reflects these paradoxes and seismic cultural shifts.

Inextricably linked to all the most important historical and political events in Russia since the 13th century, the Kremlin was the residence of the Great Prince and also a religious centre. Today, the Kremlin remains as alluring and enigmatic as ever. Two thirds of the citadel territory are closed to visitors, but the remaining third contains enough treasures to occupy several days of sightseeing. Not only is it one of the largest and most interesting museums in the world, it is also the official residence of the President of the Russian Federation. The Kremlin is the perfect place to begin your tour of Moscow.

Suzdal

Suzdal, a must-see if you can visit only one place outside of Moscow. If you have only one place to visit near Moscow. In 1864, local merchants failed to coerce the government into building the Trans-Siberian Railway through their town. Instead it went through Vladimir, 35km away. As a result Suzdal was bypassed not only by trains, but by the 20th century altogether. This is why the place remains the same as it did at the end of the 19th century — its cute wooden cottages mingling with golden cupolas that reflect the river, which meanders sleepily through gentle hills and flower-filled meadows.

As it happens, Suzdal served as a royal capital when Moscow was a mere cluster of cowsheds. It transformed into a major monastic centre during the time of Ivan the Terrible (Ivan IV Vasilyevich) and became and important commercial hub later on. Nowadays, it seems perfectly content in its retirement from both business and politics.

Obtaining a Russian Visa

According to the Russia - Israel bilateral agreement, citizens of the State of Israel do not need visas to travel to Russia for a period of up to 90 days unless the purpose of their visit is employment, education or commercial activity.

Nearly all foreigners are required to obtain a visa before entering Russia. Mauri recommends you apply for your visa one month before your departure to avoid any unforeseen complications. There are two options for securing your visa.

  • Use a Russian Visa Centre in your country (if one exists)

Details of the application process vary depending on where you apply and what kind of visa you need. Nevertheless, you will need the following for all visas:

Passport Valid for at least six months beyond your return date.
Photos One or two passport-sized photos
Completed application form Allow some time for this
Handling fee Usually in the form of a money order; amount varies.
Visa-support letter Provided by hotel, travel agent or online service.

Please find all detailed information and the set of required documents at the following links or or check the relevant information for your own country:

  • For USA citizens: www.ils-usa.com
  • For UK citizens: www.ru.vfsglobal.co.uk
  • For Canadian citizens: www.canada-ils.com
  • For German citizens:www.vhs-germany.com

Apply personally at the Russian consulate
Find your local consulate and go prepared with the set of required documents.

Please, note that there are no Russian Visa Centres in Australia. Australian citizens can find information about visas on the website of the Russian Consulate: https://www.sydneyrussianconsulate.com/en/visa-section/tourist-visa.html
